Research Focus

We study how immune resistance emerges across pancreatic primary tumors and liver metastases, then connect those maps to functional and therapeutic testing.

We aim to build a laboratory where rigorous mouse models, high-resolution omics, and mechanistic validation are used together, not separately. The long-term goal is to understand why metastatic pancreatic cancer remains resistant, and how we can turn that knowledge into smarter immunotherapy and cell-therapy strategies.

About the PI

Feng Guo, M.D.

Feng Guo is a surgeon-scientist in pancreatic disease research whose work bridges pancreatic surgery, tumor immunology, functional genomics, and translational therapeutic design.

His scientific training began at Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, and later expanded from clinical pancreatic surgery into mechanistic cancer research. His current research vision centers on pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, especially immune resistance, liver metastasis, and how to develop therapies that remain effective inside hostile metastatic niches.

This planned lab will bring together in vivo screening, single-cell and spatial profiling, epigenomic analysis, and engineered immune cell strategies to uncover actionable vulnerabilities in advanced pancreatic cancer.

Research Directions

Questions that connect mechanism to therapy

In vivo CRISPR/Cas9 screens for immune resistance

We want to know which genes help pancreatic tumors survive immunotherapy inside a living organism, where cancer cells, immune cells, and stroma constantly influence one another.

Instead of testing one gene at a time, we use pooled CRISPR screening in vivo to discover resistance genes at scale, then move the strongest hits into mechanism studies and combination-therapy design.

Orthotopic models Anti-PD-1 response Functional validation

Single-cell and spatial comparison of primary tumors versus liver metastases

Primary pancreatic tumors and liver metastases may carry the same driver mutations, but they do not live in the same immune ecosystem. We study how macrophages, fibroblasts, hepatocytes, and T cells are reorganized across those sites.

By combining single-cell sequencing with Visium HD and spatial domain analysis, we aim to identify what makes liver metastases especially difficult to control.

scRNA-seq Visium HD Niche mapping

CAR-T strategies that also reshape the tumor microenvironment

Solid tumors resist CAR-T therapy not only because cancer cells are hard to kill, but also because the surrounding microenvironment suppresses T-cell function.

We are interested in dual-target or microenvironment-aware CAR-T approaches that attack the tumor while simultaneously weakening the local immunosuppressive niche.

Platforms

Technologies we plan to integrate

In vivo functional genomics

CRISPR/Cas9 perturbation directly inside mouse tumor models to identify genes that shape therapy response under physiological immune pressure.

Single-cell transcriptomics

Cell-state resolution for tumor, immune, and stromal populations, with special focus on lineage plasticity and treatment-induced remodeling.

Spatial transcriptomics

Visium HD and downstream domain analysis to determine where immune activation or suppression occurs within intact tissue architecture.

ATAC-seq and CUT&Tag

Epigenomic mapping to understand how therapy rewires chromatin accessibility and transcriptional control in resistant tumor states.

Mouse metastasis models

Orthotopic, portal vein, liver metastasis, and other disease-relevant models that capture the biology of metastatic spread and niche adaptation.

Therapeutic engineering

Rational design of combination therapy and next-generation cell therapy informed by tumor ecology rather than tumor cells alone.
